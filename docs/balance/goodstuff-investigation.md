# The Goodstuff Investigation — a dominant cross-deck strategy, and what it means

_Session findings, 2026-07-10/11. Companion handoff for a game-design session:
[`design-handoff.md`](design-handoff.md)._

## TL;DR

A deck hand-/machine-built from the **strongest cards across all 7 premade decks** ("goodstuff")
beats every premade deck **~88% under RefereeBot** and was **confirmed unbeatable by a strong human**
piloting the premades. A double-oracle search found **no predator** for it; the top of the metagame
is a monoculture of near-identical value piles. This is not a tuning bug — it is a **structural
property of an open-construction card game with no cost and no faction axis**. Fixing it needs a
*design* intervention, not card nerfs. One prototype intervention (buffing Colony into a genuine
synergy deck) was started and is promising but unfinished.

## How it started

The experiment: hand-pick the 14 strongest designs across all decks and see how they fare. It
snowballed into an automated deckbuilding + metagame-analysis investigation.

## What we found

1. **Goodstuff dominates.** An optimizer (hill-climb over legal 4-4-6 decklists, scored by win rate
   vs the 7-deck field) converged on a "premium standalone value" pile — big apex bodies + efficient
   removal + card-draw engines. It beat the field **95% under GreedyBot, 88% under RefereeBot** (200
   games/matchup, out-of-sample). The worst matchup was still ~82%.

2. **Human-confirmed.** The user (the strongest pilot on record, ~73 Elo above RefereeBot) piloted
   each premade against bot-goodstuff and was **obliterated**. So it is *not* a bot artifact / the
   referee under-piloting synergy decks — the edge is real under human play.

3. **No predator exists (that we could find).** A double-oracle loop (`metagame_search.py`): each
   round build the best deck vs the current set, add it, repeat. Every round found *another value
   pile* (82–87% vs field); a dedicated counter-search against goodstuff could only reach **22%**.
   The replicator dynamics over the resulting 12-deck matrix collapse to a **monoculture**. The top
   of the metagame is a cluster of near-identical piles, not rock-paper-scissors.

4. **The over-powered core is ~11 cards, and they are action-economy breakers.** Five independently
   searched piles all converged on: **Polar Bear, Chinchilla** (in every pile), then Bat, Lemming,
   Tiger, Greywhisker, Black Bear, Alpha, Mouse, Raven, Rhinoceros. The theme: **cards that
   manufacture extra actions/tempo** (Chinchilla = extra action; Greywhisker = extra play; Alpha =
   3 bodies/action; Lemming = many placements/action) + premium unconditional bodies + free removal.
   In a game whose only resource is the action, action-generators are unconditionally busted.

5. **The pool is deep.** After removing goodstuff's 14 cards, an optimizer still built a **53.7%
   deck (referee) from the leftovers** ("leftover1"). So it is not "14 broken cards" — there are
   ~40+ generically strong cards, enough for multiple winning disjoint decks.

6. **The premades have dead cards (human insight).** Playing them by hand surfaced low-floor filler
   the sim can't flag: Cheetah/Rat (aggro), Fig Tree/Watering Hole (ramp) — "never happy to draw."
   Goodstuff's strength is the mirror image: **every card has a high floor, no dead draws**.

## Root cause — the "goodstuff theorem"

Given four ingredients, "pick the best cards" is a *forced* dominant strategy, unfixable by tuning:
1. a **private deck** you build,
2. from an **open pool** (any card + any card),
3. with **no cost** to play a card,
4. and cards that **aren't all equal**.

Every card game that has this problem removes one ingredient: colors/classes (#2), mana/cost (#3),
or drafting/shared-supply/fixed-decks (#1). Flattening card power (#4) is infeasible and was shown
insufficient (small edges compound over 14 picks; a global "draw 2 per action" tweak was **absorbed**
— it helped goodstuff as much as synergy). This game currently has **neither a deckbuilding
constraint nor an in-game cost** — uniquely exposed.

## Interventions considered (and their verdicts)

- **Nerf the 11 cards** — rejected. Nerf them and the search finds the next 11 (pool depth).
- **Global rule tweak (draw-2 per action)** — tested, **absorbed** (goodstuff owns the best draw
  engines + higher card quality, so buffing draw is a rising tide). No single dial fixes a
  no-tradeoff problem.
- **Factions / colors** — rejected by the designer as "Magic with extra steps."
- **In-game cost (food-as-mana)** — rejected: too mana-like, and food is generated by *being ahead*
  (region control), so pricing cards in food creates a **snowball**. Tempo-cost (2-action cards)
  rejected as coarse / just a targeted nerf.
- **Best-of-3 disjoint roster + conquest** (winner's deck retired → must win with 2 different decks)
  — **tested** (`conquest.py`, `roster_experiment.py`). The solver confirms conquest's mechanism
  (a one-dominant-deck roster wins 97% last-hero but only ~49% conquest on toy numbers). But on the
  **real, deep pool it fails**: a concentrated roster (goodstuff + 2 optimized leftovers) still wins
  the conquest series **69.6% under referee**, because the leftovers are strong enough (leftover1 =
  53.7%) to close game two. Conquest needs a *shallow* pool to bite.

## The design direction we landed on

The designer's vision (see the handoff): **a small set of roughly-equal, mechanically-distinct
decks in a rock-paper-scissors metagame**, chosen by playstyle (a League-of-Legends "champion pool"
feel), where **pilot skill can overcome a deck disadvantage**. Goodstuff-as-a-midrange-archetype is
*fine* — it just needs to be one of several, with predators. Acceptable: **faction-by-incentive**
(tribe-count threshold payoffs that reward committing, without a hard color rule). Off-limits: mana,
Magic-style colors.

For a synergy deck to beat a goodstuff pile it must be simultaneously **(1) high-ceiling, (2)
non-decomposable** (a pile can't cherry-pick a few of its pieces — needs a steep threshold), and
**(3) consistent** (comes online reliably; a pile is zero-variance). (2) and (3) fight each other —
that's the core design difficulty. The shape that threads it: **payoffs scaling on a currency a
dedicated deck accrues reliably but a pile structurally can't** (e.g. "5+ of your tribe on board").

## Colony prototype (unfinished)

Colony already has the right shape (tribe-count thresholds), so it was the first patient. Tuned via
`config.py` overrides, measured under **TurnBot** (GreedyBot under-pilots synergy → would over-buff).

- **Baseline:** 38.4% mean vs field, **7% vs goodstuff**.
- **Crank-1** (threshold 5→4; Queen Marabunta/Honoria food 4→5 **and** strength 4→5; Falstaff 3→5;
  Vesper & Guard Hornet bonuses left alone): **53.6% mean**, but **still 21% vs goodstuff**, and it
  **lost its aggro predator** (32%→56% — dropping the threshold made it out-race aggro, killing a
  desired RPS relationship).
- **Human calibration** (user pilots crank-1 Colony vs TurnBot-goodstuff, 10 games): **3-7 (~30%)**
  — above the bot's 21% but still losing. Goodstuff remains a wall; the swarm likely gets swept by
  goodstuff's removal before it snowballs (suggests **resilience**, not more food, is the missing
  lever).

**The crank was reverted** — `cards.json`/`config.py` are back to baseline. Crank-1 numbers are
recorded here and in `results/deck_optimizer/colony_crank1.json`.

## Tooling built this session (all reusable)

- **`sim/deck_optimizer.py`** — hill-climb legal 4-4-6 decks vs a field; `--pilot`, `--config`;
  synthetic decks injected via `$AK_EXTRA_DECKS` (hook added to `decks.py`).
- **`sim/metagame_search.py`** — `counter` (best-response vs a target) and `expand` (full
  double-oracle → matchup matrix → replicator verdict) modes.
- **`sim/conquest.py`** — pure series solver (conquest vs last-hero) via fictitious-play matrix games.
- **`sim/roster_experiment.py`** — 3-deck roster conquest experiment; `--from-result`,
  `--card-winrates`.
- **`sim/measure_deck.py`** — the tuning workhorse: one deck vs a field under any pilot/config.
- Artifacts + human-cohort launchers under `results/deck_optimizer/`.

## Open questions for the design session

1. Which lever makes a synergy deck **beat** a pile — resilience (survive removal) vs speed (win
   before the sweep)? Colony crank-1 suggests food/speed alone is insufficient.
2. Can several such decks be built that **RPS each other** (each beats the pile but loses to
   something), or does every strong deck collapse toward the pile?
3. Is a **format** change (conquest + a deliberately *shallow* pool) part of the answer, or purely a
   card-design one?
4. What is goodstuff's **intended** predator archetype — go-tall, hyper-aggro-race, or a combo that
   goes over the top?
